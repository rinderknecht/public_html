In short

My research interests lie in applying formal methods, making a better life for the engineers, in a broad sense, and considering information systems and telecommunication technologies.

In keywords: XML databases (XQuery), document processing (XSLT), telecommunication protocols, specification-based test generation, theoretical aspects of programming languages (e.g., type systems), symbolic computing, functional and logic programming, static analysis, compilers.

My courses were under the complementary fields of industrial software engineering and theoretical computer science. Since then, I always tried to fertilise the former with the latter. I moved to INRIA (French national research institute on informatics), in Rocquencourt, to start my Ph.D. under the supervision of Michel Mauny, in the team which developped both the functional programming language OCaml and the proof assistant Coq (now two groups: Gallium and TypiCal). In partnership with CNET (now Orange Labs), I formalised the specification language ASN.1, in use in the specifications of telecommunication protocols, especially oriented towards managing network equipments. Previous attempts were based on the specification language language Z, which is based on the powerful Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory. Instead, I provided an operational approach which lead more easily to a specification analyser in OCaml. The working prototype was used in the industry for some time and the thesis included theoretical results about its features and analyses (as syntatic completeness and correctness of the Basic Encoding Rules). During this period I represented France at the ISO working committee on ASN.1 (London, 1997) with Michel Mauny. I finished this work in 1998. I taught computer science and mathematics to undergraduates during these years.

I worked at Alcatel-Alsthom Research (now Alcatel-Lucent R&I) from 1997 to 1998, where I conducted case studies for operational subsidiaries about telecommunication software quality.

Snapshots

Didactics of Programming Languages

Recursion is a powerful programming technique which is notoriously difficult to master, especially in functional languages because they prominently feature structural recursion as the main control-flow mechanism. Juan Diego Tascón Vidarte and I propose several hypotheses to understand the issue and put some to the test by designing an open-source interactive interface based on a tangible block-world with augmented reality and software feedback. Stacks of blocks are used as an analogy for the list data structure, which enables the simplest form of structural recursion. After using this application, students are expected to transfer their training to directly write recursive programs in sequential Erlang, a purely functional language.

I also worked alone on novel ways to introduce undergraduates to recursive programming, either by relying on functional languages and logic or the analysis of algorithms. I am currently working on a survey of the field of teaching and learning recursion. (See publications for the bibliography.)

XML-related languages

I am currently interested in XML technologies, from a language/database/web perspective with tool implementation in mind. In other words: semantics of XPath, XSLT, XQuery and the pair RDF/OWL (semantic web); understanding the expressiveness of XML Schema, Relax NG and Schematron as specifications enabling to check the correctness of XML transformations or node selections; querying XML databases with XQuery; frameworks for building and maintaining large web sites; searching the semantic web. Practically, I focus mainly on creating new algorithms or algorithms which are more efficient, as well as implementing them into end-user tools.

Metacompilation

In 2007, I did some research with Dr Nic Volanschi on metacompilation. Metacompilation consists in extending compilers or, more generally, parsing-based tools, with user-defined patterns to be matched against the source code. Our novel approach consists in unparsed patterns, a kind of pattern which does not require to be parsed, hence does not require the parser of the object language to be extended, thus rewritten, in order to cope with metavariables.

Formal design review

Some commonly used languages or protocols have no formal design but we trust them because they are widely deployed and discussed among experts. Nevertheless, security issues are reaching a wider audience than ever before and thus some formal design reviews are useful to check in details whether we should really trust or not some technology. It is not a matter of providing a full formalisation and theorems, just doing some reasonable and formal works on the selected topics.

Semantics of formal languages (case study: ASN.1)

Many languages are defined by means of international standards but lack of formal foundations. This is the case of ASN.1 (Abstract Syntax Notation One) which is a standard language for defining data types whose values may be exchanged across a network between two communicating applications, independently from the possible heterogeneity of the peers. ASN.1 has been adopted by a wide range of applications, such as network management, secure email, mobile telephony, voice over IP etc. It offers a very involved subtyping paradigm consisting of constraints upon recursive types, which restrict their sets of values in a set-theoretic manner or in a structural way. Because of this great expressiveness, most ASN.1 compilers are not likely to fully check arbitrary combinations of subtyping constraints. We are interested in fully validate ASN.1 specifications and compile it by means of different theoretical approaches, like set constraints and structured operational semantics, a formalism usually employed in programming languages theory.

Test generation for telecommunication protocols

Testing telecommunication protocols is important and difficult. Here we mean protocol specifications, not the software which implements it. These specifications can be expressed in different formal languages, as SDL, LOTOS, Lustre etc., based on different theoretical models (synchronous communication, finite state machines etc.). I worked on SDL (Semantics Description Language) specifications while aiming at test generation, especially embedded testing. The problem is that it is impossible to explore all the states of a specification (and then prove some invariant property) because of the exponential number of states. Moreover, sometimes we want to cover only a given part (module) of a specification which has neither observation nor control points with the environment.
