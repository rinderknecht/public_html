Syllabus of the Logic Circuit Design course

Summary

The goal of this course is to provide the students with the notions of encoding in the context of numeration, to convey the algorithms which operate efficiently on these encodings and how these algorithms can be represented by a combination of logic circuits (gates). It will be insisted upon the mathematical justification of all the algorithms and their correctness. This course relies heavily on programming homeworks (in C), which are checked during class every week.

Plan

    • Decimal encoding of numbers revisited
    • Binary encoding of numbers
    • Most Significant Bit (MSB), Least Significant Bit (LSB)
    • Conversion from binary to decimal encoding numbers (the naive way and my means of Hörner factorisation)
    • Conversion from decimal to binary
    • Exercises
    • Octal representations of numbers
    • Conversion from octal to binary
    • Conversion from binary to octal
    • Conversion from decimal to octal (the direct way or by means of the binary encoding)
    • Exercises
    • Hexadecimal encoding of numbers
    • Conversion from hexadecimal to octal
    • Conversion from octal to hexadecimal
    • Conversion from binary to hexadecimal
    • Conversion from hexadecimal to binary
    • Conversion from decimal to hexadecimal
    • Conversion from hexadecimal to decimal
    • Exercises
    • Addition on the decimal encoding of numbers revisited
    • Subtraction on decimals revisited
    • Addition and subtraction on binary encodings (notion of carry and borrow)
    • Multiplication on binary encodings
    • Exercises
    • Two-complement binary encodings
    • The interval of values is asymmetric because of 0.
    • There is no sign bit, no MSB and no LSB
    • If the leftmost bit is 1, the number is negative.
    • If the leftmost bit is 0, the number is positive.
    • Exercises
    • Addition on two-complement encodings
    • How to detect underflow and overflow
    • Exercises
  8. Mid-term examination
    • Negation of two-complement encodings
    • Relationship with one-complement
    • Depending on how the binary operations are done, an intermediate overflow can or cannot occur.
    • Implementation in C of common arithmetic operations on two-complement encodings
    • Gray codes
    • Binary-encoded decimals (BCD)
    • Addition of BCDs
    • Subtraction
    • Implementation in C of addition of BCDs
    • Ternary encoding
    • Balanced ternary encoding
    • Conversion from ternary to balanced ternary
    • Conversion from balanced ternary to ternary
    • Implementation in C
    • Conversion from decimal to balanced ternary
    • Conversion from balanced ternary to decimal
    • Implementation in C
    • Basics of Boolean algebra
    • Identities
    • Commutative and distributive laws
    • Absorption law, logic adjacency
    • De Morgan law
    • Truth table
    • Min-terms, max-terms of a boolean expression
    • Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF), Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)
    • Exercises
    • Karnaugh maps for simplifying boolean expressions (closed and open)
    • Lots of exercises
    • Logic formulas as abstract syntax trees
    • Proving tautologies by means of the contradiction method
    • Exercises
  16. Final examination

Prerequisites

None.

Textbook

This course does not require any book in particular, as the focus is mainly in doing lots of short homeworks.

Evaluation

Two examinations as usual, plus homeworks twice a week. Exercises will be solved during class-time and answers posted on the web site. Attendance will be checked and taken into account for the final grade.
